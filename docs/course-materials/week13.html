  • 🎯 Goals

Explore your results:

  • Preprocessing: How to identify outliers to have a clean dataset?

  • Explorations: How to check unexpected results?

A brief summary of this week’s new functions:

  • plot(): Generates scatter plots and boxplots.
  • length(): Returns the number of elements or items in a vector or factor level.
  • subset(): Filters a data frame to return only the rows or columns that meet specific conditions.
  • c(): Combine/Concatenate function; used here to group column names into a vector.
  • unique(): Removes duplicate rows or elements from an object.
  • table(): Creates contingency tables to show the counts/frequencies of factor levels.
  • aggregate(): Splits data into subsets and computes summary statistics (used to find the mean reaction time per participant).
  • droplevels(): Drops unused levels from a factor variable, usually after subsetting data.
  • ave(): Computes sub-group statistics (like group means or standard deviations) and returns a vector matching the original data length.
  • sd(): Calculates the standard deviation of a set of numbers.
  • nrow(): Counts the total number of rows in a data frame.
  • as.data.frame(): Converts an object (like a table) into a standard data frame format.
  • summarySE(): Gives a summary of the data (count, mean, SD, SE) based on several values.

👀 Part 1: Have an overview of the results

🤔 Why it is a good idea to have an overall view of results

Even if we have an idea of what the results may look like based on our hypotheses, we should never forget that we are dealing with human behavior, and that something unexpected can happen. While our observations can (and should) be driven by our hypotheses, we need to keep an open mind!

First, we need to load the data that we have already prepared last week, in addition to the two necessary libraries:

load(file = "CHC9012_PCIbexDataPrepared.RData")

library(ez)
library(dplyr)
library(Rmisc)

We are digging into different ways to have a first idea of the results below:

  • Overall results based on the experimental design
  • Overall results by participant
  • Overall results by item
  • How to look at the background of the participants

  • We can have a first look at the ratings by condition:
plot(data3$Value ~ data3$Condition)

The plot() function gives us a boxplot.

How to read it?
  • Thick horizontal line: This is the median, indicating the mean rating.
  • Grey boxes: This is the interquartile range, indicating where the middle 50% of the ratings are.
  • Dashed lines: These are the whiskers, indicating scores outside the middle 50%.

The median ratings for Grammatical sentences is 5, and for Ungrammatical sentences 4. We would expect a wider difference, and this suggests that there is something happening with our data.

  • We can also look at the RT by condition:
plot(data3$RT ~ data3$Condition)

Can you see the dots? These are the outlier trials. We cannot see a clear pattern because of them. We can decide not to plot the outliers by specifying it as below:

plot(data3$RT ~ data3$Condition, outline = FALSE)

We cannot see great differences between the conditions.

Did you see that?

Did you remark the y axis (on the left)? This indicates that the participants took around 800 milliseconds to judge the sentences, which is quite strange. Again, this suggests that there is something weird happening with our dataset.

This is a human-like behavior.

🤯 Part 2: Identify and remove outliers

2.1. Identify and remove non-valid participants

Thanks to our first overview of the results, we know that the data from some participants are not valid. In this part, we will see how to identify and remove their data.

⚠️ Be careful

We need to be careful when removing data, especially not to remove too much! The challenge is that there is no absolute established rule. We will see in the following sections that we need to rely on converging evidence to be more confident and justify our choice.

We can use different types of evidence to identify non-valid participants:

  • Based on abnormal mean reaction time,
  • Based on shared IPs (shared device),
  • Based on the observation of specific participants.
  • Abnormal RT
  • Shared IPs
  • Look at specific participants

plot(data3$RT ~ data3$ID_1_TimeReceived, outline = FALSE)

2.2: Identify and remove non-valid trials

Once we identified and removed data from non-valid participants, we can remove outlier trials. Here is an overview of the process:

  • Remove based on absolute values
  • Remove based on relative values
  • Calculate the proportion of removed trials

This step means that we want to remove data whose RT is below or above a certain value. There is no absolute rule regarding the value to adopt, and this is very specific to each experiment. In our case, we want to remove trials whose RT is below 200 milliseconds and above 20,000 milliseconds (20 seconds).

data_OtherPart_CleanAbsolute <- subset(data_OtherPart_FullData, RT > 200 & RT < 20000)

Removing based on relative values means that we are looking at specific patterns from each participant and from each condition.

In experimental linguistics research, the usual way to go is to look at the mean RT of each condition for each participant, and then we remove trials that are too far from the mean. To do that, we remove trials which are below or above 2.5 times the deviation time from the means.

The following code illustrates how to do that:

# Calculate the mean per Participant and Condition
data_OtherPart_CleanAbsolute$m_rt <- ave(data_OtherPart_CleanAbsolute$RT, 
                                         data_OtherPart_CleanAbsolute$ID_1_TimeReceived, 
                                         data_OtherPart_CleanAbsolute$Condition, 
                                         FUN = mean)

# 2. Calculate the SD per Participant and Condition
data_OtherPart_CleanAbsolute$sd_rt <- ave(data_OtherPart_CleanAbsolute$RT, 
                                          data_OtherPart_CleanAbsolute$ID_1_TimeReceived, 
                                          data_OtherPart_CleanAbsolute$Condition, 
                                          FUN = sd)

# 3. Subset the data to remove outliers
data_OtherPart_CleanAbsoluteRelative <- subset(data_OtherPart_CleanAbsolute, 
                                               RT >= (m_rt - 2.5 * sd_rt) & 
                                                 RT <= (m_rt + 2.5 * sd_rt))

Finally, to make sure that we did not remove too many trials.

FullData_NbTrials <- nrow(data_OtherPart_FullData)
TrimmedData_NbTrials <- nrow(data_OtherPart_CleanAbsoluteRelative)

# Number of trimmed trials
NbTrimmedTrials <- FullData_NbTrials - TrimmedData_NbTrials
NbTrimmedTrials
[1] 14
# Proportion (%)
NbTrimmedTrials*100/FullData_NbTrials
[1] 4.666667

The proportion is below 5%, which is acceptable in experimental linguistics.

📊 Part 3: Analyses and explorations of the clean results

Now that we have a very clean dataset, we are ready to look at the results of our experiment.

  • 3.1: Design-based analyses
  • 3.2: Exploratory analyses

This is a simple experiment, including grammatical and ungrammatical sentences. The ungrammatical sentences were created by placing the negation marker ‘not’ in non-canonical places within the sentence.

Therefore, we may expect that grammatical sentences are accepted, while ungrammatical sentences are rejected.

plot(data_OtherPart_CleanAbsoluteRelative$Value ~ data_OtherPart_CleanAbsoluteRelative$Condition)
summarySE(data_OtherPart_CleanAbsoluteRelative,
          measurevar = "Value",
          groupvars = "Condition")
      Condition   N    Value       sd        se        ci
1   Grammatical 144 6.361111 1.304168 0.1086807 0.2148283
2 Ungrammatical 142 2.485915 2.041193 0.1712931 0.3386347
Observations

  • Grammatical condition: The boxplot shows that most of the ratings are above 6, which is expected. There are outliers, but this is very common in experimental linguistics.

  • Ungrammatical condition: While the low ratings are expected, there are more variations, as suggested by the boxplot and the higher standard deviation (SD).

plot(data_OtherPart_CleanAbsoluteRelative$RT ~ data_OtherPart_CleanAbsoluteRelative$Condition, outline = FALSE)
summarySE(data_OtherPart_CleanAbsoluteRelative,
          measurevar = "RT",
          groupvars = "Condition")
      Condition   N       RT       sd       se       ci
1   Grammatical 144 6131.653 4240.087 353.3406 698.4457
2 Ungrammatical 142 6111.190 4277.881 358.9918 709.7022

Even after cleaning the data, we can observe the same tendency. The RTs do not greatly differ accross the conditions.

Why exloring the data?

We observed more variation in the ungrammatical condition. The next step is to understand the source of such variation.

We already did our best with the participants, so it is likely to be related to the sentences.

  • Let’s first have a closer look at the Grammatical condition:
data_Grammatical <- droplevels(subset(data_OtherPart_CleanAbsoluteRelative,
                                        Condition == "Grammatical"))

plot(data_Grammatical$Value ~ data_Grammatical$ItemNo)

There are two to three sentences with slightly different ratings, but overall the ratings are quite consistent.

  • Now let’s look at the Ungrammatical condition:
data_Ungrammatical <- droplevels(subset(data_OtherPart_CleanAbsoluteRelative,
                                        Condition == "Ungrammatical"))

plot(data_Ungrammatical$Value ~ data_Ungrammatical$ItemNo)

While most of the sentences are rejected, this is not the case for some. This is the source of variation.

It could be tempting to remove them from the analyses, but this is a very bad idea. First, we have no reason to remove them. Second, they provide potentially interesting information. Especially, why are there ratings different?

To answer this question, we need to look even closer at these divergent sentences. Here is the code to do so:

# Exploration of divergent ungrammatical sentences
data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue <- subset(data_Ungrammatical, Value > 4)

data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Summary <- droplevels(subset(data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue,
                                                  select = c(ID_1_TimeReceived,
                                                             ItemNo,
                                                             Sentence)))
data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Summary <- unique(data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Summary)
data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Overview <- table(data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Summary$ItemNo)

data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Overview

31 33 34 36 37 38 43 44 47 48 50 51 52 55 57 58 
 1  1  1  1  1  2  3  1  2  1  2  4  2  2  1  1

The numbers in the upper row indicate the ID of the sentences. The numbers in the lower row correspond to the number of participants who gave a rating higher than 4.

For many outliers, only one participant gave a rating. This means that we should recruit more participants to make sure that the patterns is really stable. For the moment, we can focus on the sentences who received higher ratings by more than two participants. Here is the code:

# Identify which ungrammatical sentences
data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Overview <- as.data.frame(data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Overview)
names(data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Overview)[1] <- "ItemNo"

data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Overview <- droplevels(subset(data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Overview, Freq > 2))

data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Overview <- left_join(data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Overview, 
                                                      data_Ungrammatical, 
                                                      by = "ItemNo")
OutlierUngrammaticalSentences <- unique(data_Ungrammatical_OutlierValue_Overview$Sentence)
